# Random Forest

## 1. Ensemble Learning

Random Forest is an ensemble learning algorithm.

It combines multiple decision trees:

$$
T_1,T_2,\dots,T_B
$$

Each tree produces a prediction:

$$
h_b(x)
$$

The final prediction is obtained by aggregation.

---

## 2. Bootstrap Sampling

Given training dataset:

$$
D=\{(x_1,y_1),(x_2,y_2),..., (x_n,y_n)\}
$$

Random Forest creates multiple datasets:

$$
D_1,D_2,...,D_B
$$

Each dataset is generated by sampling with replacement.

Each decision tree is trained on one bootstrap dataset:

$$
T_b=f(D_b)
$$

---

## 3. Random Feature Selection

At each split, Random Forest randomly selects a subset of features.

Assume the total number of features is:

$$
d
$$

The candidate features are:

$$
F\subset\{1,2,...,d\}
$$

The best split is selected from:

$$
F
$$

instead of all features.

This reduces correlation between trees.

---

# 4. Decision Tree Split

## Gini Impurity

For a node containing K classes:

$$
Gini(D)
=
1-\sum_{k=1}^{K}p_k^2
$$


where:

$$
p_k=
\frac{N_k}{N}
$$


$N_k$ is the number of samples in class k.

$N$ is the total number of samples.

---

## Information Gain

The split quality can be measured by impurity reduction:

$$
Gain
=
Gini(parent)
-
\sum_i
\frac{|D_i|}{|D|}
Gini(D_i)
$$


The split with maximum gain is selected.

---

# 5. Random Forest Classification

For classification tasks:

Each tree predicts:

$$
h_b(x)
$$


The final prediction is majority voting:

$$
\hat{y}
=
mode
\{h_1(x),h_2(x),...,h_B(x)\}
$$


Equivalent formulation:

$$
\hat{y}
=
argmax_c
\sum_{b=1}^{B}
I(h_b(x)=c)
$$


where:

$$
I(\cdot)
$$

is the indicator function.

---

# 6. Random Forest Regression

For regression tasks:

Each tree outputs:

$$
h_b(x)
$$


The final prediction is the average:

$$
\hat{y}
=
\frac{1}{B}
\sum_{b=1}^{B}
h_b(x)
$$


---

# 7. Variance Reduction

A single decision tree has high variance:

$$
Var(T)
$$


Random Forest averages multiple trees:

$$
\hat{f}(x)
=
\frac{1}{B}
\sum_{b=1}^{B}T_b(x)
$$


The variance decreases:

$$
Var(\hat{f})
<
Var(T)
$$


because different trees are less correlated.

---

# 8. Feature Importance

Random Forest estimates feature importance by measuring the contribution of each feature.

For feature j:

$$
Importance(j)
=
\frac{
\sum \Delta Gini_j
}
{
\sum_j \Delta Gini_j
}
$$


Higher value means the feature contributes more to decision making.

---

# 9. Important Hyperparameters

## Number of Trees

$$
n\_estimators=B
$$

More trees:

- lower variance
- higher computational cost


---

## Maximum Depth

$$
max\_depth
$$

Controls tree complexity.

Large depth:

$$
\rightarrow
$$

higher variance and possible overfitting.


---

## Maximum Features

Number of features considered at each split:

$$
max\_features
$$


Common choice:

$$
\sqrt{d}
$$

for classification.

---

## Minimum Samples Split

Minimum samples required for node splitting:

$$
min\_samples\_split
$$


Higher value produces simpler trees.

---

# 10. Random Forest Algorithm

Given training data:

$$
D
$$


Repeat for:

$$
b=1,...,B
$$


1. Generate bootstrap dataset:

$$
D_b
$$


2. Train decision tree:

$$
T_b=f(D_b)
$$


3. At each split:
   
   - randomly select features
   - choose best split


4. Aggregate predictions:

Classification:

$$
\hat{y}=mode(T_1,...,T_B)
$$


Regression:

$$
\hat{y}
=
\frac{1}{B}
\sum T_b(x)
$$


---

# 11. Random Forest vs Decision Tree

| | Decision Tree | Random Forest |
|-|-|-|
|Number of trees|1|Many|
|Training data|Full dataset|Bootstrap samples|
|Feature selection|All features|Random subset|
|Variance|High|Lower|
|Overfitting|Easy|Less likely|
|Interpretability|High|Lower|

---

# 12. Random Forest vs XGBoost

| | Random Forest | XGBoost |
|-|-|-|
|Learning method|Bagging|Boosting|
|Trees|Independent|Sequential|
|Main goal|Reduce variance|Reduce bias|
|Training|Parallel|Sequential|
|Tuning difficulty|Lower|Higher|
